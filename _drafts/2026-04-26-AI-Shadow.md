## **Your Data, Their Models: How to Manage Privacy on Leading AI Platforms**

Nowadays, the use of artificial intelligence is deeply embedded in our daily lives. The AI is used across a wide range of contexts, from routine personal tasks to business operations. Major providers like Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google rely on vast amounts of data to make their models “smarter” through a continuous feedback loop of user interactions. Each interaction contributes to improving model performance, enabling more accurate, personalized, and context-aware outputs over time. However, this increasing dependence on large-scale data collection and processing introduces critical challenges and a significant tension between innovation and privacy.

In a growing digital world, how can this affect your personal and professional daily lives?

Here is a breakdown of how they handle your data and the specific steps you can take to maintain **your privacy**.

---

## **Anthropic (Claude)**
As of **August 28, 2025**, Anthropic updated its terms to allow data from **Claude Free, Pro, and Max** plans to be used for model training. This includes chats and coding sessions.
When opted-in for training, Anthropic extends its data retention period to **five years** to support long-term development. Otherwise, retention stays at the standard **30 days**.
These updates do **not** apply to business services like Claude for Work (Team/Enterprise) or API usage, which are excluded by default.
By the time of writing this article, Anthropic has already started training on data collected under the new terms, meaning that even if you opt-out now, your previous interactions may have already been used in training runs.

**How to Avoid Training:**
You can manage this at any time in your **Privacy Settings**. 

![Help Improve Claude toggle](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/claude-ai-settings.png)
*The "Help Improve Claude" toggle allows you to opt-out of training and keep your data retention at 30 days.*

## **OpenAI (ChatGPT)**
OpenAI may use content from individual services including ChatGPT, Codex, and Sora to improve model performance. This includes prompts, responses, images, and uploaded files.
By default, OpenAI does **not** train on data from **ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, or the API**. 

**How to Avoid Training (Web):**
1. Click your **profile icon** and select **Settings**.
2. Navigate to **Data Controls**.
3. Turn off **"Improve the model for everyone"**.

![ChatGPT Data Controls](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/open-ai-datacontrol-settings.png)
*The Data Controls menu in ChatGPT where you can disable model improvement.*

![Help Improve OpenAI toggle](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/open-ai-settings.png)
*The "Help Improve OpenAI" toggle allows you to opt-out of training.*

Even if you turn off the option “Improve the model for everyone,” it is important to emphasize that this does not completely prevent access to your data.

In particular, if you provide explicit feedback in the chat (like a thumbs up or down), the **entire conversation** associated with that feedback may still be used for training.

Moreover, OpenAI states that a limited number of authorized OpenAI personnel, as well as trusted service providers, may access user content. The main reasons are investigate abuse or a security incident, provide support to you if you reach out with questions about your account, handle legal matters and improve model performance (unless you have opted out). For these reasons, the company explicitly warns users not to enter sensitive information that they would not want reviewed or used.

To avoid all these risks, you can use the **Temporary Chat** mode, which ensures chats are not saved in your history, do not create memories, and are not used for training. These chats are deleted after 30 days and cannot be accessed by OpenAI for any purpose.

## **Google (Gemini)**
Gemini is deeply integrated with Google’s ecosystem, meaning it often has a larger data footprint involving your **Gmail, Calendar, and Drive** information.

**How to Avoid Training:**
1. Go to your **Gemini Apps Activity** page.
2. Under the "Gemini Apps Activity" section, click **Turn off**.
3. **Note:** Google warns that disabling this may prevent future conversations from being saved and could delete previous history.

![Google Privacy Settings](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/google-gemini-settings.png)
*Google privacy settings allow you to toggle off training.*

## **Final thoughts: from Settings to conscious Use**

The main challenge is not a single data-sharing decision, but rather the gradual accumulation of many small and often routine interactions. In everyday use of AI systems, people tend to share fragments of information without fully considering their sensitivity like copying internal documents into prompts for summarization, asking for help with work-related tasks, or providing contextual details that appear harmless in isolation. Each of these actions may seem insignificant on its own, but when combined over time, they contribute to the construction of a highly detailed digital footprint that extends across multiple platforms and services. And once our information is used in a model training run, it becomes a permanent part of that model's "memory."

This reality shifts the concept of privacy away from isolated choices such as enabling or disabling a single setting and toward ongoing behavioral awareness. Privacy is no longer something that can be “set once” and forgotten. Instead, it depends on continuous judgment in how tools are used in daily workflows. Relying exclusively on built-in toggles or platform assurances is often insufficient, particularly in professional contexts where confidentiality, intellectual property, and sensitive information are at stake.

For this reason, a more proactive and disciplined approach becomes essential.
We should develop the habit of critically evaluating what is shared with AI systems, reducing unnecessary exposure of sensitive details, and consciously distinguishing between what is safe to process and what should remain internal. When appropriate, the use of privacy-enhancing practices such as temporary chat modes, data minimization techniques, or isolated work environments can further reduce risk.

Ultimately, effective privacy in the age of AI is less about control through settings and more about consistent, informed decision-making in everyday use.
