## **Your Data, Their Models: How to Manage Privacy on Leading AI Platforms**

Nowadays, the use of artificial intelligence is deeply embedded in our daily lives. The AI is used across a wide range of contexts, from routine personal tasks to business operations. Major providers like Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft and Google rely on vast amounts of data to make their models “smarter” through a continuous feedback loop of user interactions. Each interaction contributes to improving model performance, enabling more accurate, personalized, and context-aware outputs over time.

Additionally, user interactions may not only be processed automatically but can also be subject to human review. 
The major providers featured in this article rely on human reviewers to evaluate outputs, improve quality, detect misuse, and fine-tune model behavior. This means that, depending on the service and settings, parts of your conversations could be accessed and analyzed by trained personnel and third-party contractors. While this process is typically governed by strict internal policies and safeguards, it still introduces an additional layer of exposure that users should be aware of, especially when handling  personal or professional information.

Our increasing reliance on data-intensive processes creates a fundamental tension between technological advancement and robust privacy protections.
In a growing digital world, how can this affect our personal and professional daily lives?

Below, we examine the data practices of leading providers and how we can protect our **privacy** while using their services.

## **Anthropic (Claude)**
As of **August 28, 2025**, Anthropic updated its terms to allow data from **Claude Free, Pro, and Max** plans to be used for model training. This includes chats and coding sessions.
When opted-in for training, Anthropic extends its data retention period to **five years** to support long-term development. Otherwise, retention stays at the standard **30 days**.\
These updates do **not** apply to business services like Claude for Work (Team/Enterprise) or API usage, which are excluded by default.\
By the time of writing this article, Anthropic has already started training on data collected under the new terms, meaning that even if you opt-out now, your previous interactions may have already been used in training runs.

**[How to Avoid Training](https://privacy.claude.com/en/articles/12109829-how-do-i-change-my-model-improvement-privacy-settings){:target="_blank"}:**
You can manage this at any time in your **[Privacy Settings](https://claude.ai/settings/privacy){:target="_blank"}**. 

![Help Improve Claude toggle](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/claude-ai-settings.png)
*The "Help Improve Claude" toggle allows you to opt-out of training and keep your data retention at 30 days.*

## **OpenAI (ChatGPT)**
OpenAI may use content from individual services including ChatGPT, Codex, and Sora to improve model performance. This includes prompts, responses, images, and uploaded files. It keeps your conversations, whether they are active in your sidebar or tucked away in your archives, **indefinitely** until you choose to manually remove them. It is important to realize that **simply archiving a chat does not delete it**. Archived sessions remain part of your account's standard storage. Once you actually hit delete, the conversation vanishes from your view immediately and is typically **permanently scrubbed from OpenAI's underlying systems within 30 days**, unless specific legal or de-identification exceptions apply. If you have been using connected apps, keep in mind that just disconnecting the app or archiving the session won't erase the data already pulled into those chats; you must **explicitly delete the conversation** to clear out both the chat history and any referenced app data.
By default, OpenAI does **not** train on data from **ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, or the API**. 

**[How to Avoid Training (Web)](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/7730893-data-controls-faq){:target="_blank"}:**
1. Click your **profile icon** and select **Settings**.
2. Navigate to **[Data Controls](https://chatgpt.com/#settings/DataControls){:target="_blank"}**.
3. Turn off **"Improve the model for everyone"**.

![ChatGPT Data Controls](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/open-ai-datacontrol-settings.png)
*The Data Controls menu in ChatGPT where you can disable model improvement.*

![Help Improve OpenAI toggle](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/open-ai-settings.png)
*The "Help Improve OpenAI" toggle allows you to opt-out of training.*

Even if you turn off the option “Improve the model for everyone,” it is important to emphasize that this does not completely prevent access to your data. In particular, if you provide explicit feedback in the chat (like a thumbs up or down), the **entire conversation** associated with that feedback may still be used for training.

To avoid all these risks, you can use the **Temporary Chat** mode, which ensures chats are not saved in your history, do not create memories, and are not used for training. These chats are deleted after 30 days and cannot be accessed by OpenAI for any purpose.

## **Google (Gemini)**
Gemini is deeply integrated with Google’s ecosystem, meaning it often has a larger data footprint involving your **Gmail, Calendar, and Drive** information.

**[How to Avoid Training](https://support.google.com/gemini/answer/13278892?hl=en&co=GENIE.Platform%3DAndroid#zippy=%2Cin-the-gemini-mobile-app){:target="_blank"}:**
1. Go to your **[Gemini Apps Activity](https://myactivity.google.com/product/gemini?utm_source=help){:target="_blank"}** page.
2. Under the "Gemini Apps Activity" section, click **Turn off**.
3. **Note:** Google warns that disabling this may prevent future conversations from being saved and could delete previous history.

![Google Privacy Settings](/assets/img/posts/ai-shadow/google-gemini-settings.png)
*Google privacy settings allow you to toggle off training.*

By default, the activity history is kept for **18 months**. You can change your settings to clear your history every 3 months, every 36 months, or keep it forever.

If you turn off your activity history to opt out of data collection, Google will still hold onto your conversations for a **72-hour window**.\
However, if you send feedback on a specific chat, that de-identified feedback, along with that specific conversation, is kept for **three years**.

The temporary chats aren't used to train the models.
If you’re using NotebookLM, your prompts and responses disappear completely the moment you end your session.

## **Microsoft (Copilot)**

Microsoft employs a data management strategy for its generative AI models, focusing on **analyzing large-scale patterns to improve model reasoning, language diversity, and safety systems**. \
For personal account users, the company utilizes data from interactions with **Bing, MSN, Copilot, and advertisement interactions**, which includes de-identified search results, voice activity, and any images or files you upload.

**How to avoid training:** If you are signed in with a **personal Microsoft account**, you can specifically [opt out](https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/topic/microsoft-copilot-privacy-controls-8e479f27-6eb6-48c5-8d6a-c134062e2be6#bkmk_pc3){:target="_blank"}** of allowing your conversation activity and voice interactions to be used for generative AI training.\
The specific steps depend on the platform you are using:

* On **copilot.com**: Select your **profile icon**, select your **profile name**, then select **Privacy > Training on conversation activity** and **Training on voice conversations**.

* In **Copilot for Windows or macOS**: Select your **profile icon**, then select **Settings > Privacy > Training on conversation activity** and **Training on voice conversations**.

* In the **Copilot mobile app**: Open the **menu**, select your **profile icon**, then select **Account > Privacy > Training on conversation activity** and **Training on voice conversations**.

*   **In the Edge Sidebar:** Use the sidebar menu (...), go to **Settings**, then **Memory**, where you can specifically manage **"Microsoft usage data"** to control if data from Bing, MSN, and Edge is used to personalize your experience.

Certain types of data are automatically excluded from generative AI training. For instance, if you are signed in with an organizational Entra ID account (work or school), or if you are using Copilot features integrated directly into Microsoft 365 consumer apps like Word, Excel, or PowerPoint, your data is automatically excluded.\
By default, conversation history is stored for **18 months**, though you can delete individual items or your entire history at any time. 

## **From settings to conscious use** 

The main challenge is not a single data-sharing decision, but rather the gradual accumulation of many small and often routine interactions. In everyday use of AI systems, we tend to share fragments of information without fully considering their sensitivity like copying internal documents into prompts for summarization, asking for help with work-related tasks, or providing contextual details that appear harmless in isolation. Each of these actions may seem insignificant on its own, but when combined over time, they contribute to the construction of a highly detailed digital footprint that extends across multiple platforms and services. 

The decision to opt out of AI model training should be driven by the reality that once information is shared, it becomes **integrally fused into the model’s mathematical weights**, making it technically difficult and computationally expensive to truly "forget" or "unlearn" specific data points. While these models are designed to learn general language patterns rather than specific entries, they can exhibit **memorization**, which poses a risk where the AI might inadvertently reproduce sensitive personally identifiable information (PII), such as internal documentation, when triggered by a specific context. This privacy concern is compounded by the fact that many providers employ **human reviewers** (including third-party providers) to analyze subsets of user interactions to improve accuracy, meaning that any confidential information entered could potentially be seen by another person.

From a security perspective, contributing data to these models expands the attack surface for sophisticated informatic risks, such as **inference attacks or context extraction**, where malicious actors attempt to reconstruct parts of the training data or session history. According to OWASP, one of the main threats posed by generative models is the unintentional disclosure of sensitive data. Its LLM02:2025 Sensitive Information Disclosure classification identifies this as a critical risk, particularly in environments where AI has access to personal data, intellectual property, or confidential documentation.\
Furthermore, the **extended data retention periods** required for training, which can last up to five years for certain services, significantly prolong the window of vulnerability, making stored user data a long-term target for potential data breaches and system failures. 

As we are moving into an era of what we could define as **Data Protectionism**, failing to opt out can impact Sovereign AI efforts, potentially turning a company's unique intelligence into a utility controlled by a single global entity while simultaneously threatening the traditional ad-revenue models of original content creators.
